Multiplying Polynomials

Multiplying polynomials​

The algorithm that allows us to multiply polynomials in O(nlog⁡n)O(n \log n)O(nlogn) is called the Cooley-Tukey fast Fourier transform, or FFT for short. It was discovered by Gauss 160 years earlier, but then separately rediscovered and publicized by Cooley-Tukey.

The heart of Cooley-Tukey FFT is actually about converting between coefficient and evaluation representations, rather than the multiplication itself. Given polynomials ppp and qqq in dense coefficient representation, it works like this.

  1. Convert ppp and qqq from coefficient to evaluation form in O(nlog⁡n)O(n\log n)O(nlogn) using Cooley-Tukey FFT
  2. Compute r=p∗qr = p*qr=p∗q in evaluation form by multiplying their points pairwise in O(n)O(n)O(n)
  3. Convert rrr back to coefficient form in O(nlog⁡n)O(n\log n)O(nlogn) using the inverse Cooley-Tukey FFT

The key observation is that we can choose any nnn distinct evaluation points to represent any degree n−1n - 1n−1 polynomial. The Cooley-Tukey FFT works by selecting points that yield an efficient FFT algorithm. These points are fixed and work for any polynomials of a given degree.
