Commitments

A "commitment scheme" is a cryptographic scheme that lets us provide a "commitment" to a given piece of data, in such a way that we can later "open" that commitment.

I recommend checking out section 2.4.1 of David's book Real-World Cryptography. There are two properties we typically expect of our commitment schemes:

  • Hiding. A commitment to a piece of data should not reveal anything about that data.

  • Binding. There should be no ambiguity about what data is committed to. That is, it should not be possible for the committer to open the commitment to a piece of data other than the one they originally committed to.

There are various kinds of commitments that allow opening in different ways, revealing only part of the data, or some function of the data. Sometimes it is even useful to elide the hiding property entirely--- so-called non-hiding commitments.

Simple commitments​

The simplest kind of a commitment is one in which opening reveals all of the underlying data. Let's give a simple construction of such a scheme for committing to field elements. Suppose we have

  • Fp\mathbb{F}_pFp​ a prime order field, with ppp being rather large (say on the order of 22562^{256}2256).

  • A hash function h ⁣:List(Fp)→Fph \colon \mathsf{List}(\mathbb{F}_p) \to \mathbb{F}_ph:List(Fp​)→Fp​.

Then we define

commit(x,r)=h([x,r])open(c)=(x,r)verify(c,x,r)=true/false\mathsf{commit}(x, r) = h([x, r]) \\ \mathsf{open}(c) = (x, r) \\ \mathsf{verify}(c, x, r) = \mathsf{true/false} \\commit(x,r)=h([x,r])open(c)=(x,r)verify(c,x,r)=true/false

The argument rrr of the commit\mathsf{commit}commit function is data that must only be known to the committer (until the commitment is opened). When a committer wants to commit to a field element xxx, they sample a random "blinder" r∈Fpr \in \mathbb{F}_pr∈Fp​ and hash it together with xxx to form the commitment.

To open the commitment ccc, they simply provide the committed value together with the blinder. Alternatively, if the verifier already knows xxx, they can just provide rrr, i.e. open(x,c)=r\mathsf{open}(x,c) = ropen(x,c)=r. Finally, given the commitment and the opening, we can verify whether the input was the value originally committed to using the verify\mathsf{verify}verify function.

If the hash function is collision-resistant, then this function is binding (because there's no way the committer could find another preimage of h([x,r])h([x, r])h([x,r])).

If the hash function is one-way, then this commitment is also hiding (assuming xxx is revealed only as part of the opening).

Commitments are often used in protocols between provers and verifiers. The following illustration provides an example with a prover named Peggy and a verifier named Victor.

Peggy Victor inputblinder }→commit⟶commitment  ⋮      commitment→open⟶input,blinder  ⋮    commitmentinputblinder }→verify→true/false\begin{array}{lcll} \textbf{Peggy} & \textbf{ } & \textbf{Victor} & ~ \\ \begin{aligned}\mathsf{\small input} \\ \mathsf{\small blinder}\end{aligned} ~\Bigg\} \rightarrow \boxed{\mathsf{\small commit}} & \longrightarrow & \mathsf{\small commitment} & ~ \\ ~ & \vdots & ~ & ~ \\ ~ & ~ & ~ & ~ \\ \mathsf{\small commitment} \rightarrow \boxed{\mathsf{\small open}} & \longrightarrow & \mathsf{\small input, blinder} & ~ \\ ~ & \vdots & ~ & ~ \\ ~ & ~ & \begin{aligned}\mathsf{\small commitment} \\ \mathsf{\small input} \\ \mathsf{\small blinder}\end{aligned} ~\Bigg\} \rightarrow \boxed{\mathsf{\small verify}} \rightarrow \mathsf{\small true/false} \end{array}Peggyinputblinder​ }→commit​  commitment→open​  ​ ⟶⋮ ⟶⋮ ​Victorcommitment  input,blinder commitmentinputblinder​ }→verify​→true/false​      ​

Here Peggy commits to an input using a blinder, obtains the commitment and sends it to Victor. The interlocutors continue their protocol, but eventually to convince Victor of her claims, Peggy must send the opening proof to her earlier commitment. Victor verifies the opening (i.e. the input and blinder) against the commitment. If the verification fails, then Victor knows that Peggy was trying to trick him, otherwise Victor has sufficient assurances that Peggy was telling the truth.

Algebraic and homomorphic commitments​

Instead of a cryptographic hash function, we can use elliptic curve scalar multiplication to construct a commitment scheme. Here scalar multiplication is used like a one-way function based on the hardness assumption of the elliptic curve discrete logarithm problem (ECDLP). Suppose we have

  • Fp\mathbb{F}_pFp​ a prime order field, with ppp being large (e.g. something like 22562^{256}2256).
  • Publicly agreed generator point GGG over an elliptic curve E(Fp)E(\mathbb{F}_p)E(Fp​)
  • Another publicly agreed curve point HHH for which no one knows the discrete logarithm
commit(x,r)=xG+rHopen(c)=(x,r)\mathsf{commit}(x, r) = xG + rH \\ \mathsf{open}(c) = (x, r)commit(x,r)=xG+rHopen(c)=(x,r)

where x∈Fpx \in \mathbb{F}_px∈Fp​ is the value being committed to, r∈Fpr \in \mathbb{F_p}r∈Fp​ is a random blinding factor and the commitment c=commit(x,r)c = \mathsf{commit}(x, r)c=commit(x,r) is a curve point.

These commitments are algebraic (i.e. they do not use a boolean-based cryptographic hash function) and have homomorphic properties: you can add commitments together to form another commitment of the added committed values. For example, if you have commitments AAA and BBB, you can perform:

A+B=commit(xa,ra)+commit(xb,rb)=xaG+raH+xbG+rbH=(xa+xb)G+(ra+rb)H=commit(xa+xb,ra+rb)\begin{aligned} A + B &= \mathsf{commit}(x_a, r_a) + \mathsf{commit}(x_b, r_b) \\ &= x_aG +r_aH + x_bG + r_bH \\ &= (x_a + x_b)G + (r_a + r_b)H \\ &= \mathsf{commit}(x_a + x_b, r_a + r_b) \end{aligned}A+B​=commit(xa​,ra​)+commit(xb​,rb​)=xa​G+ra​H+xb​G+rb​H=(xa​+xb​)G+(ra​+rb​)H=commit(xa​+xb​,ra​+rb​)​

In other words, the sum of commitments AAA and BBB is equal to the commitment of the sum of the two committed values xax_axa​ and xbx_bxb​ and blinders rar_ara​ and rbr_brb​. This is possible because in such a scheme scaling is commutative with adding scalars.

As a cryptographic primitive, the ability to find a public curve point HHH for which no one knows the discrete logarithm may, at first, seem rather mind-blowing and powerful.

Actually, it's as easy as it is awesome to find such a point--- simply perform rejection sampling by cryptographically hashing GGG (or, respectively, the hash output), using the output as the xxx-coordinate of a candidate point on EEE and checking whether it's valid. The first valid curve point obtained is HHH and by the hardness assumption of the ECDLP, no one knows it.

Since approximately half of the hash outputs will be valid curve points on EEE, sampling will terminate very quickly. Indeed, as we will see later, this process can be used to sample many public curve points G1,…,GnG_1, \ldots, G_nG1​,…,Gn​ for which the discrete logarithms are unknown; the so-called hash to curve algorithm.

Pedersen commitments​

The homomorphic commitment commit(x,r)=xG+rH\mathsf{commit}(x, r) = xG + rHcommit(x,r)=xG+rH described above is known as a Pedersen commitment. If you remove the rHrHrH term you get a non-hiding commitment, called a Pedersen hash. Both rely on the ECDLP hardness assumption.

This means that, at least theoretically, you might be lucky (or have a quantum computer) and figure out that H=hGH = hGH=hG, which would allow you to find different values x′x'x′ and h′h'h′ to open the commitment. We say that pedersen commitments are computationally binding and not unconditionally binding. For example, you could express c=xG+rHc = xG + rHc=xG+rH alternatively as c=xG+rhG=(x+rh)Gc = xG + rh G = (x + rh) Gc=xG+rhG=(x+rh)G and compute a satisfying opening pair x′=rhx' = rhx′=rh and r′=xhr' = \frac{x}{h}r′=hx​.

On the other hand, Pedersen commitments are unconditionally hiding, as there is no way (even with a magic computer) to reveal what xxx is without knowing rrr. Lack of perfect binding is the reason why most of the "proofs" we will see later in this book are not referred to as proofs, but instead are referred to as arguments of knowledge (although we may care little about this distinction). Just remember that you need perfect binding to be called a proof.

Interestingly, it is impossible to have a commitment scheme that has both perfect hiding and perfect binding.

To recap, in cryptography the following distinctions are important

  • Perfect. The property that an algorithm is statistically sound without hardness assumptions, also known as unconditional or statistical soundness.

  • Computational. The algorithm relies on a hardness assumption or computational limitation for soundness.

Thus, said another way, Pedersen commitments provide perfect hiding and computational binding.

Vector commitments​

We can commit to several values x1,⋯ ,xnx_1, \cdots, x_nx1​,⋯,xn​ by sending separate Pedersen commitments to all of these values as such:

x1G+r1H,⋮xnG+rnHx_1 G + r_1 H, \\ \vdots \\ x_n G + r_n H \\x1​G+r1​H,⋮xn​G+rn​H

But we can instead batch/aggregate all of these commitments together as a single commitment:

x1G1+⋯+xnGn+rHx_1 G_1 + \cdots + x_n G_n + r Hx1​G1​+⋯+xn​Gn​+rH

with G1,⋯ ,Gn,HG_1, \cdots, G_n, HG1​,⋯,Gn​,H independent bases with unknown discrete logarithms.

If you represent xxxs and the GGGs as two vectors x⃗=(x1,⋯ ,xn)\vec{x} = (x_1, \cdots, x_n)x=(x1​,⋯,xn​) and G⃗=(G1,⋯ ,Gn)\vec{G} = (G_1, \cdots, G_n)G=(G1​,⋯,Gn​), we can quickly write the previous statement as an inner product

x⃗G⃗+rH\vec{x}\vec{G} + rHxG+rH

Vector commitments (sometimes referred to as multi-commitments) are a powerful construction because an arbitrarily large vector can be committed with a single curve point.

The naive approach to constructing an opening proof for a length nnn vector commitment has size O(n)O(n)O(n). It is simply the tuple (x1,…,xn,r)(x_1, \ldots, x_n, r)(x1​,…,xn​,r). As we will see later, opening proofs for vector commitments is an interesting topic and there is a much more efficient algorithm.

Polynomial commitments​

To construct SNARKs we use polynomial commitments. A polynomial commitment scheme for a field FFF (or it could even be a ring) is a way of committing to a polynomial f∈F[x]f \in F[x]f∈F[x] to get a commitment ccc, in such a way that for any α∈F\alpha \in Fα∈F, you can provide y=f(α)y = f(\alpha)y=f(α), along with an "opening proof" π\piπ that proves that the polynomial committed to in ccc equals yyy when evaluated at α\alphaα.

In other words, it is a type of commitment CCC, a type of randomness RRR, a type of opening proof PPP along with algorithms

commit ⁣:F[x]×R→Copen ⁣:C×F×(F[x]×R)→F×Pverify ⁣:C×(F×P)→Bool\mathsf{commit} \colon F[x] \times R \to C \\ \mathsf{open} \colon C \times F \times (F[x] \times R) \to F \times P \\ \mathsf{verify} \colon C \times (F \times P) \to \mathsf{Bool}commit:F[x]×R→Copen:C×F×(F[x]×R)→F×Pverify:C×(F×P)→Bool

such that for any f∈F[x],  r∈R,  α∈Ff \in F[x],\; r \in R,\; \alpha \in Ff∈F[x],r∈R,α∈F , we have

c:=commit(f,r)verify(c,open(c,α,(f,r)))=truec := \mathsf{commit}(f, r) \\ \mathsf{verify}(c, \mathsf{open}(c, \alpha, (f, r))) = \mathsf{true}c:=commit(f,r)verify(c,open(c,α,(f,r)))=true

and if b≠f(α)b \neq f(\alpha)b=f(α) then it is not possible to compute π∈P\pi \in Pπ∈P such that

verify(c,(b,π))=true\mathsf{verify}(c, (b, \pi)) = \mathsf{true}verify(c,(b,π))=true

In other words, if b≠f(α)b \neq f(\alpha)b=f(α) then every π\piπ which is feasible to compute results in verify(c,(b,π))=false\mathsf{verify}(c, (b, \pi)) = \mathsf{false}verify(c,(b,π))=false.

Constructing polynomial commitment schemes​

All known constructions of polynomial commitment schemes are a bit complicated. The easiest to describe is called the Kate (pronounced "kah-TAY") scheme, also known as "KZG". It requires a "prime-order group with a pairing", which is three groups G1,G2,GTG_1, G_2, G_TG1​,G2​,GT​ of prime order ppp (hence, all isomorphic cyclic groups) together with a function e ⁣:G1×G2→GTe \colon G_1 \times G_2 \to G_Te:G1​×G2​→GT​ such that for any a1,a2∈Za_1, a_2 \in \Za1​,a2​∈Z, g1∈G1g_1 \in G_1g1​∈G1​, g2∈G2g_2 \in G_2g2​∈G2​, we have

e(a1⋅g1,a2⋅g2)=a1a2⋅e(g1,g2)e(a_1 \cdot g_1, a_2 \cdot g_2) = a_1 a_2 \cdot e(g_1, g_2)e(a1​⋅g1​,a2​⋅g2​)=a1​a2​⋅e(g1​,g2​)

eee is called a "pairing" or a "bilinear pairing". What this lets us do is "multiply in the scalar" but only once.

Fix a degree bound ddd on the polynomials we would like to be able to commit to. The KZG scheme, will let us commit to polynomials in Fp[x]<d\mathbb{F}_p[x]_{< d}Fp​[x]<d​. As a preliminary, fix generators g1∈G1,g2∈G2g_1 \in G_1, g_2 \in G_2g1​∈G1​,g2​∈G2​ arbitrarily.

The first thing to know about the KZG scheme is it requires that we randomly sample some group elements to help us. This is the dreaded and much discussed trusted setup. So, anyway, we start by sampling τ\tauτ at random from Fp\mathbb{F}_pFp​ and computing for i<di < di<d,

hi:=(τi)⋅g1w:=τ⋅g2h_{i} := (\tau^i) \cdot g_1 \\ w := \tau \cdot g_2hi​:=(τi)⋅g1​w:=τ⋅g2​

And then throw away τ\tauτ. The security depends on no one knowing τ\tauτ, which is sometimes referred to as the toxic waste of the trusted setup. Basically we compute the generator scaled by powers of τ\tauτ up to the degree bound. We make a security assumption about the groups which says that all anyone can really do with group elements is take linear combinations of them.

Now suppose we have a polynomial f∈Fp[x]<df \in \mathbb{F}_p[x]_{<d}f∈Fp​[x]<d​ with f=∑i<daixif = \sum_{i < d} a_i x^if=∑i<d​ai​xi that we would like to commit to. So cfc_fcf​ is g1g_1g1​ scaled by f(τ)f(\tau)f(τ) and the fact that G1G_1G1​ is an Fp\mathbb{F}_pFp​-module (i.e. a vector space whose scalars come from Fp\mathbb{F}_pFp​) means we can compute f(τ)⋅g1f(\tau) \cdot g_1f(τ)⋅g1​ from the hih_{i}hi​ and the coefficients of fff without knowing τ\tauτ.

Now how does opening work? Well, say we want to open at a point aaa to b=f(a)b = f(a)b=f(a). Then the polynomial f−bf - bf−b vanishes at aaa, which means that it is divisible by the polynomial x−ax - ax−a (exercise, use polynomial division and analyze the remainder).
