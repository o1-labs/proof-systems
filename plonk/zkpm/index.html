A More Efficient Approach to Zero Knowledge for PLONK

In PLONK (considered as an interactive oracle proof), the prover sends the verifier several polynomials. They are evaluated at some kkk points during the course of the protocol. Of course, if we want zero-knowledge, we would require that those evaluations do not reveal anything about the proof's underlying witness.

PLONK as described here achieves zero knowledge by multiplying the polynomials with a small degree polynomial of random coefficients. When PLONK is instantiated with a discrete-log base Bootle et al type polynomial commitment scheme, the polynomial degrees must be padded to the nearest power of two. As a result, since several of the polynomials in PLONK already have degree equal to a power of two before the zero-knowledge masking, the multiplication with a random polynomial pushes the degree to the next power of two, which hurts efficiency. In order to avoid it, we propose an alternative method of achieving zero knowledge.

Zero Knowledge for the Column Polynomials​

Let www be the number of rows in a PLONK constraint system. For a typical real-world circuit, www will not be equal to a power of two.

Let the witness elements from one column be s1,s2,…,sws_1, s_2, \ldots, s_ws1​,s2​,…,sw​. Let nnn be the closest power of two to www such that n≥wn \geq wn≥w. Let F\mathbb{F}F be the field that witness elements belong to.

Now, in vanilla PLONK, we pad the sis_isi​ with n−wn - wn−w elements, interpolate the polynomial over a domain of size nnn, scale it by a low degree random polynomial, and commit to the resulting polynomial. We want to avoid increasing the degree with the scaling by a low degree polynomial, so consider the following procedure.

Procedure. Sample kkk elements uniformly from F\mathbb{F}F: rw+1,…,rw+kr_{w+1}, \ldots, r_{w+k}rw+1​,…,rw+k​. Append them to the tuple of witness elements and then pad the remaining n−(w+k)n - (w+k)n−(w+k) places as zeroes. The resulting tuple is interpolated as the witness polynomial.
